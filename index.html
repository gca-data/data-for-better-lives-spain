Data for Better Lives in Spain

A Proposal to Build a National Culture of Data

1. How Data Is Transforming Development in Spain

Spain is undergoing a profound digital transformation driven by mobility data, health information systems, financial platforms, and satellite imagery. Data already contributes to better decision-making in several key sectors:

  • ✔ Mobility and transport efficiency (Madrid, Barcelona)
    Real-time mobility data from buses, metros, and navigation apps helps optimize transport flows and manage congestion.
  • ✔ Climate and wildfire management
    Earth observation data is used by the Generalitat and national agencies to predict fire risk, water scarcity, and other climate-related threats.
  • ✔ Health system modernization (SNS & autonomous regions)
    The digitalization of medical records improves coordination across levels of care, especially in remote and aging regions.
  • ✔ Tourism and economic planning
    Private intent data from hotels, booking platforms, and telecoms helps understand seasonal dynamics and local economic activity.

Spain already has the data. The next step is ensuring that public sector leadership has the literacy, confidence, and institutional support needed to turn these data assets into better policies.

2. The Challenge: Spain Still Lacks a Strong, Coherent Data Culture

Despite progress, several barriers still limit Spain’s ability to fully leverage its data ecosystem:

  • • Fragmentation across autonomous communities
    Health, transport, and education data often remain siloed due to governance and interoperability gaps between regions and institutions.
  • • Uneven levels of data literacy among senior officials
    Many leaders continue to rely on traditional reports rather than real-time analytics or structured partnerships with private data holders.
  • • Limited reuse of private intent data
    Telecom mobility data, platform transaction data, and digital payments remain underutilized for policy design and evaluation.
  • • Insufficient public understanding and trust
    Citizens express concerns about privacy, algorithmic bias, and how government institutions use their data.
  • • Lack of a central institution to coordinate data governance
    Multiple ministries and agencies have overlapping responsibilities without a unified framework or clear leadership on data governance.

Spain has enormous potential, but it needs a clear, coordinated national strategy for building a culture of data across its public institutions.

3. Proposed Solution: Create a National Data Leadership Program for Spain

The National Data Leadership Program (NDLP) would build Spain’s capacity to use data ethically, effectively, and strategically across all levels of government.

  • 1️⃣ High-Level Training for Senior Leadership
    Equip ministers, secretaries-general, and regional leaders with skills to:
    • Understand modern data sources (public and private intent data).
    • Interpret analytics for decision-making.
    • Encourage responsible experimentation and innovation.
    • Ensure privacy and ethical use of data in line with GDPR.
    This program would be practical, case-based, and aligned with the Spanish public administration context.
  • 2️⃣ A Unified National Data Governance Framework
    Spain needs a coherent, nationwide data governance strategy that includes:
    • Interoperability standards across autonomous communities.
    • Clear legal frameworks for responsible public–private data sharing.
    • Privacy-by-design principles consistent with GDPR.
    • Unified oversight under a dedicated National Data Authority.
    This framework will build consistency, trust, and data portability across the country.
  • 3️⃣ Partnerships With Universities, Startups, and Large Data Holders
    Spain has world-class institutions (CSIC, universities, Telefónica, BBVA, Santander) that already analyze large datasets. The government can:
    • Collaborate on mobility, financial inclusion, and climate models.
    • Negotiate privacy-safe access to anonymized mobility and platform data.
    • Support evidence-based regional development policies.
    This ensures data-driven innovation benefits all regions, not just Madrid and Barcelona.
  • 4️⃣ National Open-Data Platform 2.0
    Spain’s open-data portals are strong but can be modernized to:
    • Include more real-time datasets.
    • Add visual dashboards for citizens and decision-makers.
    • Integrate regional and municipal datasets.
    • Allow secure researcher access through APIs.
    This will reinforce transparency, reuse, and public trust.
  • 5️⃣ Applied “Learning by Doing” Pilots
    Pilot projects can be deployed in:
    • Wildfire prediction and climate risk.
    • Hospital emergency optimization.
    • Poverty mapping in rural areas.
    • Coastal erosion and climate planning.
    • Public transport redesign in smaller cities.
    These concrete applications will help institutions internalize the value of data and build lasting capacity.

4. Policy Recommendations for Spain

  • ✔ Policy 1: Recognize Data as a Strategic Public Asset
    Integrate data-driven decision-making into national development plans and budget processes.
  • ✔ Policy 2: Establish a National Data Governance Authority
    Centralize oversight, improve coordination across autonomous communities, and ensure ethical and consistent data use.
  • ✔ Policy 3: Strengthen Data Skills Across All Levels of the Public Sector
    A modern state requires leaders and frontline workers who are confident users of data, supported by ongoing training and institutional incentives.
  • ✔ Policy 4: Promote Safe Access to Private Intent Data
    Enable partnerships with telecoms, banks, and digital platforms to support public-good projects under strict privacy and security safeguards.
  • ✔ Policy 5: Build Public Trust Through Transparency and Communication
    Explain clearly how data is used, ensure robust privacy protections, and demonstrate tangible benefits for citizens across Spain.
