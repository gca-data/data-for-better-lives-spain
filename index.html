Data for Better Lives in Spain

A Proposal to Build a National Culture of Data

1. How Data Is Transforming Development in Spain

Spain is undergoing a profound digital transformation driven by mobility data, health information systems, financial platforms, and satellite imagery. Data already contributes to better decision-making in areas such as:

✔ Mobility and transport efficiency (Madrid, Barcelona)

Real-time mobility data from buses, metros, and navigation apps optimizes transport flows.

✔ Climate and wildfire management

Earth observation data is used by the Generalitat and national agencies to predict fire risk and water scarcity.

✔ Health system modernization (SNS & autonomous regions)

The digitalization of medical records improves service coordination, especially in remote and aging regions.

✔ Tourism and economic planning

Private intent data from hotels, booking platforms, and telecoms helps understand seasonal dynamics and local economic activity.

Spain has the data. The next step is ensuring the public sector leadership has the literacy, confidence, and institutional support needed to turn these data assets into better policies.

2. The Challenge: Spain Still Lacks a Strong, Coherent Data Culture

Despite progress, several barriers still limit the country’s ability to fully leverage its data ecosystem:

  • Fragmentation across autonomous communities
    Health, transport, and education data often remain siloed due to governance and interoperability gaps.
  • Uneven levels of data literacy among senior officials
    Many leaders rely on traditional reports rather than real-time analytics or private data partnerships.
  • Limited reuse of private intent data
    Telecom mobility, platform transaction data, or digital payments remain underutilized for policy design.
  • Insufficient public understanding and trust
    Citizens express concerns about privacy, algorithmic bias, and the use of their data by government institutions.
  • Lack of a central institution to coordinate data governance
    Multiple ministries have overlapping responsibilities without a unified framework.

Spain has enormous potential — but needs a clear, coordinated national strategy for building a culture of data across its public institutions.

3. Proposed Solution: Create a National Data Leadership Program for Spain

The National Data Leadership Program (NDLP) would build Spain’s capacity to use data ethically, effectively, and strategically across all levels of government.

1️⃣ High-Level Training for Senior Leadership

Equip ministers, secretaries-general, and regional leadership with skills to:

  • understand modern data sources (public + private intent data);
  • interpret analytics for decisions;
  • encourage responsible experimentation; and
  • ensure privacy and ethical use.

This program would be practical, case-based, and aligned with the Spanish public administration context.

2️⃣ A Unified National Data Governance Framework

Spain needs a coherent, nationwide data governance strategy that includes:

  • interoperability standards across autonomous communities;
  • clear legal frameworks for responsible public–private data sharing;
  • privacy-by-design principles consistent with GDPR; and
  • unified oversight under a dedicated National Data Authority.

This builds consistency, trust, and data portability across the country.

3️⃣ Partnerships With Universities, Startups, and Large Data Holders

Spain has world-class institutions (CSIC, universities, Telefónica, BBVA, Santander) that already analyze massive datasets.

The government can:

  • collaborate on mobility, financial inclusion, and climate models;
  • negotiate privacy-safe access to anonymized mobility and platform data; and
  • support evidence-based regional development policies.

This ensures data-driven innovation benefits all regions, not just Madrid and Barcelona.

4️⃣ National Open-Data Platform 2.0

Spain’s open-data portals are strong but can be modernized to:

  • include more real-time datasets;
  • add visual dashboards for citizens;
  • integrate regional and municipal datasets; and
  • allow secure researcher access through APIs.

This reinforces transparency and public trust.

5️⃣ Applied “Learning by Doing” Pilots

Pilot projects in:

  • wildfire prediction;
  • hospital emergency optimization;
  • poverty mapping in rural areas;
  • coastal erosion and climate planning; and
  • public transport redesign in smaller cities.

These concrete applications help institutions internalize the value of data.

4. Policy Recommendations for Spain

✔ Policy 1: Recognize Data as a Strategic Public Asset

Integrate data-driven decision-making into national development plans and budget processes.

✔ Policy 2: Establish a National Data Governance Authority

Centralize oversight, improve coordination across autonomous communities, and ensure ethical data use.

✔ Policy 3: Strengthen Data Skills Across All Levels of the Public Sector

A modern state requires leaders and frontline workers who are confident users of data.

✔ Policy 4: Promote Safe Access to Private Intent Data

Enable partnerships with telecoms, banks, and digital platforms to support public-good projects under strict safeguards.

✔ Policy 5: Build Public Trust Through Transparency and Communication

Explain clearly how data is used, ensure privacy protections, and demonstrate real benefits for citizens.
